Our idea: looking at NO2 data to track the change in range of movements, especially fossil fuel based transportation such as cars and buses. NO2 is natural occurring gas, but is also produced in the combustion of fossil fuels. Therefore it can be considered an indicator of density of population, as well as the range of distance people move (i.e daily commutes).

By comparing changes in NO2 with real-life data, we can estimate the density of population and how far people move

During COVID-19 Quarantines, we can more accurately pinpoint areas of population density due to the decreased movement of population. Then by comparing data from different time periods, we can determine the extent to which population mobility correlates to COVID-19 cases.
Above is the data for COVID-19 and NO2 in Spain over the same time frame (1st Jan - 31st May). As you can see, there’s a clear inverse correlation between the levels; High levels of NO2 tended to be the prelude to high rates of covid cases (accounting for the 2-3 week incubation period for the virus).
