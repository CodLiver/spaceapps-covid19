Our idea

Looking at NO2 data to track the change in range of movements, especially fossil fuel based transportation such as cars and buses. NO2 is natural occurring gas, but is also produced in the combustion of fossil fuels. Therefore it can be considered an indicator of density of population, as well as the range of distance people move (i.e daily commutes).

By comparing changes in NO2 with real-life data, we can estimate the density of population and how far people move

During COVID-19 Quarantines, we can more accurately pinpoint areas of population density due to the decreased movement of population. Then by comparing data from different time periods, we can determine the extent to which population mobility correlates to COVID-19 cases.

Graphs for COVID and NO2

Above is the data for COVID-19 and NO2 in Spain(right) over the same time frame (1st Jan - 31st May), as well as for the UK(left). As you can see, there appears to be an inverse correlation, but this is actually clarified as a delayed positive correlation when accounting for the 2-3 week incubation period for coronavirus. High levels of NO2 tended to be the prelude to high rates of covid cases (accounting for the 2-3 week incubation period for the virus).

Explanation of process

We attempted several different types of data (night lights, vegetation)processing on different data with varying levels of success.

For the data processing, we have used Python/OpenCV to analyze the NO2 changes respect to country regions. we have overlayed Sentinel satelite's NO2 levels heatmap with real world map. We have applied red filter to distinguish high level NO2 changes.

Then we have decided to integrate with other real life data to increase the added value such as lockdown dates, and COVID-19 daily cases respect to their countries In the end, we have observed that the high NO2 levels significantly decrease since the COVID-19 outbreak and reached the all month lows when the lockdown measures are added such as Spain.

Final Analysis and conclusion

NO2 levels and COVID-19 have a positive correlation. Spikes in NO2 levels tend to result in higher covid cases in the area in the following weeks (accounting for the incubation period). The likely reason for this is the increased movement of the population resulting in more people coming into contact with each other.

We can also see the effects of different lockdown approaches on effect in CO2. Areas with heavy lockdown see much more drastic lowering of NO2 in general (i.e the effect of Spain’s later, more intense lockdown). A good quarantine procedure should effectively reduce NO2 emissions, as the population shouldn’t be mobile. It’s also a good indicator that quarantine works. We can also improve our estimations by including other data types such as CO2 and night lights to make up for the limitations of NO2.

Future possibilities and applications of findings

We can use our findings, and improve our process to help make more informed decisions on population movement during future quarantines. By developing better NO2 monitoring, we can use it as a tool to assist the tracking of how well populations are adhering to quarantine so that appropriate steps can be taken to mitigate negative effects of pandemics.

We can also use the data to estimate “range” of different populations movement, and use this to help determine possible at-risk areas of infection.
